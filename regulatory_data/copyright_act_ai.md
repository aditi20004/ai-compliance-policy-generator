# Copyright Act 1968 and Artificial Intelligence

Source: Copyright Act 1968 (Cth)

## Overview

Australian copyright law requires a human author for copyright protection to subsist. This creates significant uncertainty around AI-generated content and the use of copyrighted material as AI training data. SMEs using AI to generate marketing copy, code, images, or other content face both ownership gaps and infringement risks.

## Key Provisions for AI Governance

### AI-Generated Content and Authorship

- Under the Copyright Act 1968, copyright subsists in original literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works created by a **human author** (ss 32-35).
- Purely AI-generated content — where no human has exercised sufficient creative control — is unlikely to attract copyright protection in Australia.
- This means AI outputs may enter the **public domain** immediately, leaving SMEs unable to prevent competitors from copying AI-generated work.
- Content with **substantial human creative input** augmented by AI tools is more likely to be protectable, though the threshold remains legally untested.

### AI Training on Copyrighted Material

- Using copyrighted works (text, images, code, music) as training data for AI models may constitute **reproduction** under s 31, potentially infringing copyright.
- Australia's fair dealing exceptions (ss 40-42) are narrower than US fair use and may not cover large-scale AI training ingestion.
- The Copyright Act's exceptions for research/study (s 40), criticism/review (s 41), and parody/satire (s 41A) are unlikely to apply to commercial AI training at scale.
- SMEs using third-party AI tools should assess whether the tool provider has lawful rights to the training data.

### IP Ownership of AI Outputs

- If an employee uses AI to produce work, ownership depends on the degree of human authorship — not merely who prompted the AI.
- Contracts with freelancers and contractors should explicitly address ownership of AI-assisted outputs.
- AI tool terms of service often contain clauses affecting output ownership; these must be reviewed carefully.

## 2024 Government Consultation

- In 2024, the Australian Government conducted a public consultation on **AI and copyright**, examining whether legislative reform is needed.
- Key issues under consideration include:
  - Whether a new category of protection for AI-generated works is warranted.
  - Whether a specific exception for AI training (text and data mining) should be introduced.
  - How to balance incentives for human creators with innovation in AI.
- As of early 2026, no legislative amendments have been enacted, and the existing Copyright Act framework applies.

## SME Relevance

- **Do not assume AI outputs are protected**: Content generated entirely by AI may not be copyrightable. Consider adding substantial human creative contribution to secure protection.
- **Audit AI tool licences**: Review terms of service for AI tools (e.g., ChatGPT, Midjourney, GitHub Copilot) to understand IP ownership and usage rights for outputs.
- **Training data risk**: If building or fine-tuning AI models, ensure training data is licensed or falls within a copyright exception.
- **Contractual safeguards**: Update employment contracts, freelancer agreements, and procurement contracts to address AI-generated content ownership.
- **Labelling**: Consider maintaining records of which outputs are AI-generated versus human-authored to support future ownership claims.

## Penalties

- **Copyright infringement**: Civil remedies include injunctions, damages, and account of profits (ss 115-116).
- **Criminal infringement**: Commercial-scale infringement carries penalties of up to 5 years imprisonment and/or 850 penalty units for individuals (s 132AC-132AI).
- **Conversion damages**: Infringing copies may be seized and destroyed (s 116).
- **Note**: Penalties apply to infringement of others' copyright when using AI, not to the absence of copyright in AI outputs.
