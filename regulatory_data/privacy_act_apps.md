# Privacy Act 1988 — Australian Privacy Principles (APPs)

## Overview

The Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) regulates how personal information is handled by Australian Government agencies and private sector organisations with an annual turnover of more than $3 million (and some smaller organisations).

The Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) are the cornerstone of the privacy protection framework in the Privacy Act. They apply to organisations and agencies covered by the Act.

## Key APPs Relevant to AI Use

### APP 1 — Open and Transparent Management of Personal Information

Organisations must manage personal information in an open and transparent way. This includes having a clearly expressed and up-to-date privacy policy about how they manage personal information.

**AI Relevance:** Organisations using AI to process personal information must disclose this in their privacy policy, including what AI tools are used and how personal information flows through them.

### APP 2 — Anonymity and Pseudonymity

Organisations must give individuals the option of not identifying themselves, or of using a pseudonym, unless impracticable or required by law.

**AI Relevance:** When using AI tools, consider whether personal identifiers are necessary. Anonymise or pseudonymise data before AI processing where possible.

### APP 3 — Collection of Solicited Personal Information

Organisations must only collect personal information that is reasonably necessary for their functions or activities. Sensitive information has additional restrictions.

**AI Relevance:** Only feed AI tools the minimum personal information necessary. Do not collect additional personal information solely for AI training or enhancement.

### APP 5 — Notification of Collection of Personal Information

Organisations must notify individuals about certain matters when collecting personal information, including the purposes of collection.

**AI Relevance:** If personal information will be processed by AI tools, individuals should be notified of this fact and the purpose.

### APP 6 — Use or Disclosure of Personal Information

Personal information can only be used or disclosed for the primary purpose of collection, or a directly related secondary purpose the individual would reasonably expect.

**AI Relevance:** Using personal information as input to AI tools may constitute a secondary use requiring consent or meeting another exception under APP 6.

### APP 8 — Cross-Border Disclosure of Personal Information

Before disclosing personal information to an overseas recipient, organisations must take reasonable steps to ensure the overseas recipient does not breach the APPs.

**AI Relevance:** Many AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) are hosted overseas. Using these tools with personal information constitutes cross-border disclosure. Organisations must ensure the AI provider's data handling complies with the APPs.

### APP 11 — Security of Personal Information

Organisations must take reasonable steps to protect personal information from misuse, interference and loss, and from unauthorised access, modification or disclosure.

**AI Relevance:** AI tool usage introduces new vectors for data exposure. Security controls must extend to AI tool access, API keys, conversation logs, and AI-generated outputs containing personal information.

## Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) Scheme

Part IIIC of the Privacy Act establishes the NDB scheme. Organisations covered by the Act must:

1. **Assess** suspected data breaches within 30 days
2. **Notify** the OAIC and affected individuals if a breach is likely to result in serious harm
3. **Take remedial action** to prevent or reduce harm

### AI-Related Breach Scenarios

- Personal data accidentally pasted into an external AI chatbot
- AI tool's data breach exposing previously input personal information
- AI-generated outputs that re-identify individuals from anonymised data
- Unauthorised access to AI conversation logs containing personal information

## Penalties

Serious or repeated interferences with privacy can result in civil penalties of up to $50 million, three times the value of any benefit obtained, or 30% of adjusted turnover (whichever is greatest).
