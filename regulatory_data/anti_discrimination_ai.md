# AI Bias and Australian Anti-Discrimination Law

Source: Age Discrimination Act 2004 (Cth), Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth), Racial Discrimination Act 1975 (Cth), Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (Cth), Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth), Australian Human Rights Commission Act 1986 (Cth)

## Overview

Australia does not yet have AI-specific anti-discrimination legislation. However, existing federal anti-discrimination laws apply to decisions made with AI assistance. When AI systems produce biased outputs in hiring, lending, insurance, or service delivery, the organisation deploying the AI — not the AI vendor — bears primary legal responsibility. SMEs using AI for decision-making must ensure outputs do not constitute direct or indirect discrimination under existing law.

## Key Provisions for AI Governance

### Racial Discrimination Act 1975

- Section 9 prohibits discrimination based on race, colour, descent, or national/ethnic origin in access to goods, services, facilities, land, accommodation, and employment.
- Section 18C prohibits conduct reasonably likely to offend, insult, humiliate, or intimidate on the basis of race.
- **AI risk**: AI systems trained on historically biased data may discriminate against applicants or customers from particular racial or ethnic backgrounds.

### Sex Discrimination Act 1984

- Prohibits discrimination on the basis of sex, sexual orientation, gender identity, intersex status, marital status, pregnancy, and family responsibilities (ss 5-7A).
- Covers employment, education, goods/services, and accommodation.
- **AI risk**: AI hiring tools may penalise career gaps (disproportionately affecting women) or favour language patterns associated with male candidates.

### Age Discrimination Act 2004

- Prohibits discrimination on the basis of age in employment, education, access to premises, and provision of goods and services (ss 14-39).
- **AI risk**: AI screening tools may use proxies for age (graduation year, years of experience) that result in unlawful age discrimination.

### Disability Discrimination Act 1992

- Prohibits discrimination on the basis of disability, including physical, intellectual, psychiatric, sensory, and learning disabilities (ss 5-10).
- Requires reasonable adjustments to avoid discrimination (s 5(2)).
- **AI risk**: AI systems may fail to accommodate disabilities — for example, automated interview platforms that disadvantage candidates with speech or hearing impairments.

### Fair Work Act 2009

- Section 351 prohibits adverse action (dismissal, demotion, refusal to hire) on the basis of protected attributes including race, sex, age, disability, religion, and political opinion.
- **AI risk**: AI-driven performance monitoring, redundancy selection, or hiring decisions that correlate with protected attributes may constitute adverse action.

### Australian Human Rights Commission Act 1986

- Establishes the AHRC's power to investigate and conciliate complaints of unlawful discrimination (Part II, Division 4).
- The AHRC has actively examined AI and human rights, publishing guidance on algorithmic accountability.

### Direct vs Indirect Discrimination

- **Direct discrimination**: AI explicitly treats a person less favourably because of a protected attribute.
- **Indirect discrimination**: AI applies a condition, requirement, or practice that is facially neutral but disproportionately disadvantages people with a protected attribute and is not reasonable in the circumstances. This is the more common AI risk.

## SME Relevance

- **Audit AI outputs**: Regularly test AI decision-making tools for disparate impact across protected attributes (gender, age, race, disability).
- **Vendor accountability**: AI vendors do not absorb your legal liability. SMEs must independently verify that AI tools comply with anti-discrimination law.
- **Human oversight**: Maintain meaningful human review of AI-assisted decisions in hiring, lending, insurance, and customer service.
- **Documentation**: Record the basis for AI-assisted decisions to support a defence if a discrimination complaint is made.
- **Training**: Ensure staff using AI tools understand that discriminatory AI outputs expose the organisation to liability.
- **Complaints process**: Complaints may be lodged with the AHRC, state/territory anti-discrimination bodies, or the Fair Work Commission. Conciliation is typically attempted before litigation.

## Penalties

- **AHRC complaints**: The AHRC facilitates conciliation; if unresolved, complaints may proceed to the Federal Court or Federal Circuit Court.
- **Court remedies**: Include compensation (uncapped for economic loss), declarations, injunctions, and orders requiring policy changes.
- **Fair Work Act**: Adverse action claims can result in compensation, reinstatement, and civil penalties of up to $93,900 per contravention for corporations.
- **Reputational harm**: Discrimination findings — even settled complaints — carry significant reputational risk for SMEs.
- **Note**: Ignorance that an AI system was biased is not a defence. Liability attaches to the discriminatory outcome, not the intent.
