# Privacy and Other Legislation Amendment Act 2024 (POLA Act)

Royal Assent: 10 December 2024. Implements 23 of 106 proposals from the Attorney-General's Privacy Act Review.

## Automated Decision-Making Transparency (APP 1.7–1.9)

**Commencement: 10 December 2026** (24-month preparation window)

New provisions require any APP entity using a "computer program" — defined broadly to encompass AI, ML, and rule-based systems — to disclose in their privacy policy:

1. The kinds of personal information used in automated processing
2. The kinds of decisions made solely or substantially by automation
3. Whether those decisions could "reasonably be expected to significantly affect the rights or interests of an individual"

### What This Means for SMEs Using AI

If your organisation uses AI for any of the following, you must update your privacy policy by December 2026:
- Automated customer service responses that affect service outcomes
- AI-assisted hiring, performance reviews, or termination decisions
- Credit scoring or lending decisions using AI
- Insurance underwriting or claims processing with AI
- Automated content moderation affecting user access
- AI-driven pricing or discount decisions
- Fraud detection systems that block transactions

**Note:** Tranche 1 only mandates transparency — it does not yet grant individuals a right to meaningful explanation of specific decisions. That right is expected in Tranche 2.

## Three-Tier Penalty Regime

The penalty structure has been fundamentally restructured:

### Tier 1: Administrative Failures
- **Up to $330,000 for corporations**
- Applies to: non-compliant privacy policies, failure to disclose automated decision-making, administrative breaches
- Enforced via infringement notices (no court proceedings required)

### Tier 2: Interferences with Privacy
- **Up to $3.3 million**
- Applies to: improper collection, use, or disclosure of personal information
- Includes failure to comply with Australian Privacy Principles

### Tier 3: Serious Interferences with Privacy
- **Up to $50 million, or three times the benefit obtained, or 30% of adjusted turnover — whichever is greatest**
- Applies to: serious or repeated interferences with privacy
- OAIC now has search-and-seizure powers and compliance notice authority

## Statutory Tort for Serious Invasion of Privacy

**Commenced: 10 June 2025**

Applies to ANY individual or organisation regardless of Privacy Act coverage (including sub-$3M businesses).

- Individuals can sue for damages up to $478,550 (non-economic and punitive)
- Covers: intrusion upon seclusion and misuse of private information
- No small business exemption — applies universally
- An SME employee uploading customer data to an AI tool without consent could trigger this

## Small Business Exemption — Expected Removal

Currently, businesses with annual turnover under $3 million are exempt from the Privacy Act (covering ~95% of Australian businesses).

**Status as of March 2026:**
- Government agreed in principle to remove the exemption
- Scheduled for Tranche 2 (actively being developed per Attorney-General Michelle Rowland, July 2025)
- No bill introduced yet, but direction is confirmed and irreversible

**Important exceptions — these businesses are ALREADY covered regardless of revenue:**
- Businesses that "trade in personal information" (selling customer lists, data brokering, lead generation)
- Health service providers
- Businesses related to the Commonwealth
- Small businesses that have opted in

**Using AI tools that process customer data could plausibly be characterised as "trading in" personal information.**

## Tranche 2 — Expected Changes

Attorney-General Michelle Rowland confirmed (July 2025) that Tranche 2 will introduce:

1. **Broadened definition of "personal information"** — replacing "about" with "relates to," capturing IP addresses, device IDs, behavioural data, and AI-inferred data
2. **"Fair and reasonable" test** for all data handling — even consented AI processing could breach the Act if deemed unfair
3. **Removal of the small business exemption**
4. **Enhanced individual rights** including a right to erasure
5. **Right to meaningful explanation** of automated decisions

## OAIC Enhanced Powers

The POLA Act granted the OAIC:
- Search-and-seizure powers
- Compliance notice authority
- Simplified extra-territorial reach
- Privacy Commissioner Carly Kind: "2025 is going to be a big year for privacy enforcement"
- December 2025: enforcement sweep conducted
