# OAIC Guidance on AI and Privacy

Source: Office of the Australian Information Commissioner (OAIC)

## Overview

The OAIC has issued guidance on how the Privacy Act 1988 and Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) apply to organisations developing or using AI systems that handle personal information.

## Key OAIC Guidance Points

### 1. Privacy by Design in AI

Organisations should embed privacy protections into AI systems from the design phase:
- Conduct Privacy Impact Assessments (PIAs) before deploying AI systems
- Minimise the collection and use of personal information in AI training and operation
- Build in technical safeguards such as anonymisation, encryption, and access controls
- Design AI systems to support individual rights (access, correction, deletion)

### 2. Transparency About AI Use

The OAIC expects organisations to be transparent about their use of AI:
- Inform individuals when AI is used to make decisions about them
- Explain in general terms how AI systems process personal information
- Update privacy policies to reflect AI usage
- Provide mechanisms for individuals to seek human review of AI decisions

### 3. Accountability for AI Decisions

Organisations remain accountable for decisions made with AI assistance:
- AI does not absolve organisations of responsibility under the Privacy Act
- Organisations must be able to explain AI-assisted decisions
- Human oversight mechanisms should be in place for significant decisions
- Regular audits of AI systems for compliance and accuracy

### 4. Consent and AI

When AI processing goes beyond the primary purpose of collection:
- Obtain informed consent from individuals before using their data with AI
- Ensure consent is voluntary, current, specific, and informed
- Individuals should understand what AI processing entails
- Consent mechanisms should be easy to understand and actionable

### 5. Data Minimisation in AI

The OAIC emphasises data minimisation when using AI:
- Only use the personal information necessary for the AI task
- Consider whether anonymised or de-identified data would suffice
- Delete personal information from AI systems when no longer needed
- Avoid using personal information for AI model training without appropriate authority

### 6. Cross-Border Data Flows and AI

Many AI tools process data overseas. The OAIC notes:
- Cloud-based AI services may transfer data to overseas servers
- Organisations must assess the privacy protections in the destination country
- Contractual arrangements should ensure APP compliance by overseas AI providers
- Consider Australian-hosted alternatives for sensitive personal information

### 7. Children's Data and AI

Special care is required when AI systems process children's data:
- Higher privacy standards apply
- Parental consent may be required
- AI outputs involving children must be subject to enhanced review

## OAIC Regulatory Priorities for AI

The OAIC has identified AI as a regulatory priority, focusing on:
- How organisations handle personal information in generative AI
- Whether privacy policies adequately cover AI usage
- The use of AI in automated decision-making
- Cross-border data flows through AI platforms
- Compliance with the NDB scheme for AI-related breaches

## Practical Steps for Organisations

1. **Audit** current AI tool usage and map data flows
2. **Assess** privacy risks through formal PIAs
3. **Update** privacy policies to cover AI usage
4. **Train** staff on privacy-compliant AI use
5. **Implement** technical controls for data protection in AI
6. **Document** AI decision-making processes
7. **Monitor** AI outputs for privacy compliance
8. **Review** regularly and adapt to regulatory changes
