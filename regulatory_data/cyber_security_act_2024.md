# Cyber Security Act 2024

Source: Cyber Security Act 2024 (Cth)

## Overview

The Cyber Security Act 2024, which commenced in November 2024, is Australia's first standalone cybersecurity legislation. It introduces mandatory ransomware payment reporting, security standards for smart devices, limited use protections for information shared with the Australian Signals Directorate (ASD), and establishes a Cyber Incident Review Board. The Act has direct implications for AI-dependent organisations, particularly where AI systems are targets of or involved in cyber incidents.

## Key Provisions for AI Governance

### Mandatory Ransomware Payment Reporting

- Entities with annual turnover above a prescribed threshold must report ransomware payments to the Australian Signals Directorate (ASD) within **72 hours** of making or becoming aware of the payment.
- Reports must include details of the attack, the payment amount, the cryptocurrency wallet or payment mechanism used, and the attacker's demands.
- **AI relevance**: Ransomware attacks that compromise AI systems — including model integrity, training data, or AI-driven operational systems — trigger the reporting obligation. An SME whose AI-powered service is encrypted by ransomware must report if payment is made.
- Failure to report does not criminalise the payment itself but attracts civil penalties.

### Security Standards for Smart Devices (IoT)

- The Act empowers the Minister to set mandatory cybersecurity standards for internet-connected devices sold in Australia.
- Standards may cover default passwords, software update capabilities, vulnerability disclosure, and secure communication protocols.
- **AI relevance**: AI-enabled IoT devices (smart cameras, voice assistants, autonomous sensors) must comply with these standards. SMEs manufacturing or importing AI-powered devices must monitor and implement applicable standards.

### Limited Use Provisions for ASD Information

- Information voluntarily shared with the ASD during cyber incident response is subject to **limited use obligations**.
- The ASD cannot use this information for regulatory enforcement or prosecution against the reporting entity, encouraging open information sharing.
- **AI relevance**: Organisations can share details about AI system compromises, model poisoning, or adversarial attacks with ASD without fear that the information will be used against them by other government agencies.

### Cyber Incident Review Board

- The Act establishes a **Cyber Incident Review Board** to conduct no-fault post-incident reviews of significant cybersecurity incidents.
- The Board can examine systemic vulnerabilities, recommend improvements, and publish de-identified findings.
- **AI relevance**: Major incidents involving AI system failures, adversarial manipulation, or AI-facilitated attacks may be subject to Board review. Findings may inform future AI security standards.

## SME Relevance

- **Ransomware preparedness**: SMEs should have incident response plans that include the 72-hour ransomware reporting obligation, especially for AI systems that are business-critical.
- **AI system inventory**: Maintain a register of AI systems and their cybersecurity dependencies to enable rapid reporting if an incident occurs.
- **IoT compliance**: SMEs selling or deploying AI-enabled devices must track mandatory security standards and ensure compliance before market entry.
- **ASD engagement**: The limited use protections make it safer for SMEs to engage with ASD during incidents. SMEs should include ASD contact procedures in their incident response plans.
- **Supply chain**: Assess whether third-party AI service providers have adequate cybersecurity controls and ransomware response plans.

## Penalties

- **Failure to report ransomware payment**: Civil penalties of up to 60 penalty units ($18,780 as of 2024) per contravention. Escalating penalties may apply for continued non-compliance.
- **Non-compliance with smart device standards**: Products may be prohibited from sale, recalled, or subject to infringement notices.
- **Note**: The Act deliberately avoids criminalising ransomware payments themselves to encourage transparency and reporting.
