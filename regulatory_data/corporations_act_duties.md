# Directors' Duties and AI Governance under the Corporations Act 2001

Source: Corporations Act 2001 (Cth), ss 180-184

## Overview

The Corporations Act 2001 imposes statutory duties on company directors and officers that directly engage AI governance responsibilities. As organisations adopt AI systems that create legal, financial, and reputational risks, directors must actively oversee these deployments or face personal liability. These duties apply to all Australian companies, including SMEs.

## Key Provisions for AI Governance

### Section 180 — Duty of Care and Diligence

- Directors must exercise their powers with the degree of care and diligence a reasonable person would in the same position.
- This requires directors to **understand the AI systems** their organisation deploys, including their risk profiles, data inputs, and potential failure modes.
- A director who remains wilfully ignorant of AI-related risks — such as bias, data breaches, or regulatory non-compliance — may breach s180.
- The duty extends to ensuring adequate AI governance frameworks, policies, and monitoring are in place.

### Section 181 — Good Faith and Proper Purpose

- Directors must act in good faith in the best interests of the corporation and for a proper purpose.
- Deploying AI systems without adequate risk assessment, or ignoring known AI failures, may constitute a breach of good faith.
- AI decisions that expose the company to foreseeable legal liability could be challenged under this section.

### Section 182 — Misuse of Position

- Directors must not improperly use their position to gain an advantage for themselves or others, or to cause detriment to the corporation.
- Directing AI procurement to benefit a related party without proper disclosure, or using AI-derived insights for personal gain, engages this duty.

### Section 183 — Misuse of Information

- Directors must not improperly use information obtained through their position.
- AI systems often process sensitive commercial data, personal information, and trade secrets. Directors who misuse AI-generated insights or confidential training data may breach s183.
- This also applies to information about AI system vulnerabilities or failures.

### The Business Judgment Rule (s 180(2))

A director satisfies the duty of care under s180(1) if they make a business judgment in good faith, for a proper purpose, without material personal interest, after informing themselves to the extent they reasonably believe appropriate, and rationally believe the judgment is in the best interests of the corporation. For AI governance, this means:

- Directors should **document** AI risk assessments and governance decisions.
- Seeking independent expert advice on AI risks supports a defence under this rule.
- Establishing an AI governance committee or framework demonstrates reasonable diligence.
- The rule does **not** protect directors who make no judgment at all — passive ignorance of AI risks is not protected.

## SME Relevance

- **Board-level awareness**: Even in small companies, directors must maintain a working understanding of AI systems in use and their associated risks.
- **Proportionate governance**: SMEs are not expected to have the same governance infrastructure as large corporations, but must demonstrate reasonable and proportionate oversight.
- **Practical steps**: Maintain a register of AI tools in use, conduct periodic risk reviews, document key decisions about AI adoption, and seek external advice where needed.
- **Delegation**: Directors can delegate AI oversight but retain ultimate responsibility. They must ensure delegates are competent and monitored.
- **Insurance**: Consider whether existing D&O insurance covers AI-related governance failures.

## Penalties

- **Civil penalties**: Up to $1.11 million per contravention for individuals (s 1317G).
- **Criminal offences**: Dishonest breaches of ss 181-183 carry up to 5 years imprisonment and/or criminal fines (ss 184).
- **Compensation orders**: Courts may order directors to compensate the corporation for losses caused by AI governance failures.
- **Disqualification**: ASIC may seek disqualification orders against directors who breach their duties.
